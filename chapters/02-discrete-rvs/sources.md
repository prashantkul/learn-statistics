# Chapter 2 — Sources

*Discrete Random Variables and Their Distributions*

## Blitzstein & Hwang, *Introduction to Probability* (2e)

- **Chapter 3: Random variables and their distributions** — pp. 103–148. The full chapter. Introduces the RV-as-function definition, PMF, CDF, Bernoulli, Binomial, Hypergeometric, story proofs, and independence of RVs.
  - §3.1 Random variables (pp. 104–106).
  - §3.2 Distributions and probability mass functions (pp. 106–109).
  - §3.3 Bernoulli and Binomial (pp. 109–112).
  - §3.4 Hypergeometric (pp. 113–117).
  - §3.5 Discrete uniform (pp. 117–119).
  - §3.6 Cumulative distribution functions (pp. 119–122).
  - §3.7 Functions of random variables (pp. 123–127).
  - §3.8 Independence of r.v.s (pp. 127–131).
  - §3.9 Connections between Binomial and Hypergeometric (pp. 131–134).
  - §3.10 Recap + R section (pp. 134–148).
- **Chapter 4 (targeted slices): Expectation** — we pull the distribution-definition slices only; the expectation and variance derivations are deferred to our Ch 3.
  - §4.3 Geometric and Negative Binomial (pp. 157–163) — stories, PMFs, CDF of Geometric, Negative Binomial PMF and identities.
  - §4.7 Poisson (pp. 174–180) — PMF definition, the Poisson paradigm, law-of-rare-events framing, near-birthday application.
  - §4.8 Connections between Poisson and Binomial (pp. 181–183) — the Binomial → Poisson limit, convolution of independent Poissons.

(Sections §§4.1, 4.2, 4.4, 4.5, 4.6 belong to our Ch 3 — expectation, variance, linearity, LOTUS. Sections §4.9 and §4.10 are skipped here.)

## Wasserman, *All of Statistics*

- **Chapter 2: Random variables** — pp. 19–26 (the discrete-focused portion). Compact re-derivation of the same material with a slightly different emphasis (mass functions as the default terminology, point-mass / degenerate distribution explicitly named).
  - §2.1 Introduction (pp. 19–20) — RV as function; sample-space example.
  - §2.2 Distribution functions and probability functions (pp. 21–24) — CDF, PMF, PDF in parallel.
  - §2.3 Some important discrete random variables (pp. 25–26) — Point Mass, Discrete Uniform, Bernoulli, Binomial, Geometric, Poisson (compact summary).

(Wasserman §§2.4–2.10 — continuous RVs, bivariate, marginal, conditional, independent — belong to our Ch 4.)
